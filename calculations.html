7.1.2. Cholesky decomposition¶

pyre.cholesky.CholeskyDecomposition(A)[source]¶

Cholesky Decomposition

The Cholesky decomposition of a Hermitian positive-definite matrix \({\bf A}\) is a decomposition of the form

\[\mathbf{A = L L}^{*}\]

where \({\bf L}\) is a lower triangular matrix with positive diagonal entries, and \({\bf L}^*\) denotes the conjugate transpose of \({\bf L}\). Every Hermitian positive-definite matrix (and thus also every real-valued symmetric positive-definite matrix) has a unique Cholesky decomposition.

If the matrix \({\bf A}\) is Hermitian and positive semi-definite, then it still has a decomposition of the form \({\bf A} = {\bf LL}^*\) if the diagonal entries of \({\bf L}\) are allowed to be zero.

When \({\bf A}\) has real entries, \({\bf L}\) has real entries as well.

The Cholesky decomposition is unique when \({\bf A}\) is positive definite; there is only one lower triangular matrix \({\bf L}\) with strictly positive diagonal entries such that \({\bf A} = {\bf LL}^*\). However, the decomposition need not be unique when \({\bf A}\) is positive semidefinite.

The converse holds trivially: if \({\bf A}\) can be written as \({\bf LL}^*\) for some invertible \({\bf L}\), lower triangular or otherwise, then A is Hermitian and positive definite.

Args:
  • A (mat): Hermitian positive-definite matrix \({\bf A}\)
Returns:
  • Lo (mat): Returns a lower triangular matrix \({\bf L}\) with positive diagonal entries
